SOUTH CHINA ELITE

Why this new and emerging interest in

ethical leadership?

This is how I look at it. As we are intently watching

the global markets, both Fortune 500 and Inc 500

companies continue to look at China and India as the

key major mega markets where opportunities for

market expansion are vibrantly increasing in different

corners. On the other hand, Chinese businesses are also

eyeing more aggressively companies overseas in North

America and Europe.

Looking back, while hospitality is still a key

component of doing business in Asia, the historical way

of doing business has changed from the past where

companies relied heavily upon relationships (Guanxi)

and facilitation where budgets appear to be modest.

Moving forward, the focus has shifted to more stern

corporate transparency requirements, dual listings, more

special audits and independent overseers – thus stirring

up a sharp demand for more ethical leadership not only

in China but also in the global business environment.

With recent notable leadership practice challenges

in Malaysia, the Philippines, South Korea, Brazil and

Taiwan, it’s becoming more evident that both country

and corporate leadership must become more principled

and ethical if business sustainability and change are

to be achieved. The recent cases of Volkswagen and

Mitsubishi erring in corporate and consumer disclosures

clearly reveals that the challenge has pervaded not only

in China but in the global arena as well.

How will these affect management

education?

Foremost, let’s understand that China has made

education a national priority. Almost every week a new

university opens its doors in China. Equally sobering

and compelling is that at least 40% of the world’s

engineering and technical graduates are minted each

year in China and by 2020 it is expected to be around

60%. The implication of such significant upsurge of

graduates is so compelling given the fact that the

More than ever, Management and Business degree

programs must be relevant and practical, since the

library of congress now almost doubles itself every four

years. Towards that end, it’s even more important that

graduate business education must be taught by leading

practitioners together with experienced faculty. In

recent years, many EMBA programs have come under

heavy criticism as many participants have been

enrolling for the purpose of networking and for future

access. Needless to say, this is unfair, if not awkward.

This motive has not considered the many benefits

participants may have enjoyed and that they may

have eventually applied whatever significant learning

they would gain in building business development

initiatives and facilitating business from Hong Kong to

Shanghai to Singapore along with many other key cities

in the region.

Thus, that leading EMBA and DBA programs need

to be responsive to market needs and conditions has

become more of a compelling necessity than a mere

option in management education. As the demands

grow, emerging special programs such as Conflict

Management, Business and Behaviour, Global Trends

and Social Network Theory will definitely emerge. As

such, learning for all of us has no other option than to

be perpetual and lifelong.

Leadership education obviously is not effective

if attended once every 20 years. Study of leadership

should not be restricted to a discussion about the latest

fads and models but must go deeper into the anatomy

of leadership coupled with a profound understanding

of Behavioural Science. All too often, leaders today are

promoted within corporations not because of their

innovative or motivational abilities and skills but

because of their politically savvy and manoeuvring. To
