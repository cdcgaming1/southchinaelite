SOUTH CHINA ELITE

Since 2003, Hong Kong residential prices have been

on an uptrend. It has registered stronger growth at

+10.5% compound annual growth rate (CAGR) in the last

decade (2006 to 2016) versus the previous decade (1996

to 2006) of -3.7%. It has beaten the inflation rate of +3.2%

CAGR and the Hang Seng Index of +1.0% consistently.

Apart from low interest rates (close to zero Fed rate)

since 2009, a favorable undersupplied market and robust

domestic/investment demand have been the key reasons.

From a demographic perspective, new immigrants,

non-local students, graduates and employees have been

driving the demand for private residential units. In 2015,

more than 56,000 people obtained work-related visas.

About 30% of the 45,000 Chinese immigrants in 2016 had

“one-way permits” to stay in private units. Meanwhile,

15,000 non-local students (the highest ever) enrolled in

university programs in 2015/16. Many of these received

permanent residency (PR) status after being in Hong

Kong for seven years, including their university stay, and

do not have to pay additional stamp duty for purchasing

a residential unit.

Abundant liquidity and high local investment

demand are other driving forces. Investors accounted

for 40% of private residential sales in 2011. This ratio

reduced to 30% in 2016 after introduction of double

stamp duty and buyers stamp duty, and further reduced

to around 10% of sales volume after the government

raised the double stamp duty rate again in November

2016. Despite the decline in the number of foreigners

buying units, we see local home buyers use the loophole

to purchase multiple units using single sale and purchase

agreements to avoid heavy stamp duty. Hong Kong

remains an aspirational place to own property for

mainland Chinese.

Hong Kong has been experiencing a shortage of

private housing in the past 10 years or so, especially

as the number of persons per household is decreasing.

Private residential stock has grown at a CAGR of +0.8%

in last decade, quite similar to population growth at

+0.7% per annum but much lower than +1.1% domestic

household growth. Despite the government increasing

land supply in the last four years, completions lagged but

now the supply of completed units is expected to reach

17,000 to 18,000 in 2017, much higher than the average

of 12,000 in the last five years.
